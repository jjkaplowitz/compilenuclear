1940s Speed

The Lost Art of Bold Testing

Nuclear testing has ground to a halt. Reactors were built and rapidly deployed in hours to a few years in the 1940s-1960s. Today there are 30-40+ nuclear startups but zero operational test sites in the United States.

1942

Hands-On Criticality

Manhattan Project scientists built the world's first reactor under Stagg Field at the University of Chicago. Louis Slotin and others would manually position plutonium cores between neutron-reflecting bricks, adjusting criticality by hand with nothing more than a screwdriver. This wasn't recklessness—it was empirical necessity. They learned through direct experimentation what no computer could simulate.

1946

The Demon Core

The infamous "demon core" killed two scientists in separate criticality accidents. Yet these tragedies weren't from lack of care—they emerged from an era where hands-on testing was the only path to understanding. Each experiment, successful or failed, taught lessons that shaped nuclear safety forever. The speed of learning was extraordinary because testing was constant.

1950s

BORAX Experiments

The Boiling Water Reactor Experiments deliberately destroyed reactors to understand failure modes. Engineers would intentionally cause meltdowns, explosions, and runaway reactions in controlled environments. BORAX-I was intentionally destroyed in 1954 to study reactor excursions. This aggressive testing culture built the safety knowledge we rely on today.

1960s

SNAP & NERVA

NASA and the AEC tested nuclear rocket engines and space reactors with extraordinary ambition. The NERVA program ran nuclear thermal rockets through countless hot-fire tests. Small reactor programs like SNAP put nuclear power in space. The pace of iteration was relentless—because the testing infrastructure existed.
